A.L.A. Schechter Poultry Corporation et al. v. United States, 295 U.S. 495 (1935). Argued May 2-3, 1935. Decided May 27, 1935.

Defendants (Schechter Poultry Corp. et al.) were indicted in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of New York on 60 counts. After dismissals and acquittals, they were convicted on 18 counts for violating the "Live Poultry Code" and one count for conspiracy to commit such violations.

The Circuit Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit sustained the conviction on the conspiracy count and on 16 counts for code violations, but reversed the conviction on two counts which charged violations of minimum wage and maximum hour provisions (holding these were not within federal power).

Both the defendants (No. 854) and the government (No. 864, appealing the reversal of the wage/hour counts) petitioned the Supreme Court. The Supreme Court granted writs of certiorari for both cases.

The defendants were slaughterhouse operators (or "marketmen") in Brooklyn, New York. New York City is the largest live poultry market in the U.S., with 96% of its poultry coming from other states.

Defendants purchased live poultry from commission men at railroad terminals in New York and New Jersey after the poultry had completed its interstate journey. They then trucked the poultry to their slaughterhouses in Brooklyn.

At their slaughterhouses, they slaughtered the poultry and sold it *locally* to retail dealers and butchers. These sales were not in interstate commerce. The "Live Poultry Code," which they were convicted of violating, was a "code of fair competition" for the NYC metropolitan area, promulgated by the President under Section 3 of the National Industrial Recovery Act (NIRA).

The specific violations charged included:

  • Violating the minimum wage (50 cents/hr) and maximum hour (40/week) provisions for their employees.
  • Violating Article VII, Section 14 (the "straight killing" provision) by allowing retail dealers to "make selection of individual chickens taken from particular coops," rather than requiring them to purchase the "run of any half coop, coop, or coops."
  • Selling an "unfit chicken" and making false reports.

  1. Did Congress, through the NIRA, unconstitutionally delegate its legislative power to the President by authorizing him to approve "codes of fair competition" without establishing clear standards?
  2. Did the "Live Poultry Code" attempt to regulate intrastate transactions (like the defendants' local slaughtering and sales) that lay beyond the authority of Congress under the Commerce Clause?
  3. (As raised by defendants, but not decided by the Court) Were the code's provisions in certain respects repugnant to the due process clause of the Fifth Amendment?

The Supreme Court, in a unanimous decision, reversed the judgment of the Circuit Court. It held the "Live Poultry Code" invalid and reversed the conviction.

The Court found for the defendants on the two primary legal issues:

  1. Yes. The NIRA's delegation of code-making authority to the President was an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power.
  2. Yes. The code's provisions as applied to the defendants' business were an attempt to regulate intrastate commerce that only indirectly affected interstate commerce, which is beyond the constitutional reach of Congress.

I. Unconstitutional Delegation of Legislative Power

  • Chief Justice Hughes, writing for the Court, stated that Section 3 of the NIRA is "without precedent." It "supplies no standards for any trade, industry, or activity."
  • The Act does not "prescribe rules of conduct," but rather "authorizes the making of codes to prescribe them." The "Declaration of Policy" in Section 1 of the Act was deemed too broad to serve as a meaningful standard.
  • This lack of standards gave the President "virtually unfettered discretion to make whatever laws he thinks may be needed or advisable."
  • The Court held that "The Congress is not permitted to "abdicate or to transfer to others the essential legislative functions with which it is thus vested." This was not a valid delegation but an unconstitutional transfer of power.

II. Scope of the Commerce Clause

  • The Court first determined that the defendants' transactions were not "in interstate commerce". The "flow of commerce had ceased" when the poultry arrived at the Brooklyn slaughterhouses. "The poultry had come to a permanent rest within the state."
  • The Court then asked if these purely intrastate activities "directly affected interstate commerce". It found they did not.
  • It re-emphasized the "necessary and well-established distinction between direct and indirect effects." The defendants' employee wages, hours, and local sales practices had "no direct relation to interstate commerce."
  • The government's argument (that low wages/long hours affect local prices, which in turn affect the demand for poultry from other states) was rejected as proving "too much."
  • If such *indirect* effects were sufficient, "it would seem that a similar control might be exerted over other elements of cost... rents, advertising, methods of doing business... All the processes of production and distribution... could likewise be controlled."
  • The Court concluded that accepting this argument "would be to destroy the distinction... between commerce 'among the several States' and the internal concerns of a state."
  • Justice Cardozo (joined by Justice Stone) wrote a separate concurrence to emphasize his agreement, particularly on the delegation point.
  • He argued this delegation was far worse than the one struck down in *Panama Refining Co. v. Ryan* earlier that year. He wrote the power delegated here was "not canalized within banks that keep it from overflowing. It is unconfined and vagrant."
  • He famously stated this was not just a delegation to stop "unfair" practices, but to regulate all "ordinances... desirable or helpful for the well-being or prosperity of the industry." He concluded: "This is delegation running riot."
  • On the Commerce Clause, he agreed the connection was too remote. "The law is not indifferent to "considerations of degree..." To find immediacy or directness here is to find it almost everywhere. If centripetal forces are to be isolated... there will be an end to our federal system."
